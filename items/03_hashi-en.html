Two Worlds Linked by the Sound "Hashi"

The Japanese sound "hashi" carries two meanings. One refers to the indispensable chopsticks at the daily dining table. The other refers to a bridge that connects two places or worlds. This overlap of meanings—whether by chance or design—quietly reveals the profound significance of the tool called hashi. Through the act of eating, it links people to one another, people to nature, and the present to the past. Chopsticks are not merely utensils; they are themselves a "bridge" that has connected Japanese life and spirit.





About Hashikura-ji

Nestled in Miyoshi City, Tokushima Prefecture, Hashikura-ji is a historic temple with over 1,200 years of history. Known as the "God of Chopsticks," the temple hosts an annual Hashi Kuyo (Chopstick Memorial Service), where visitors bring their worn-out chopsticks to express their deep appreciation. The reverence and gratitude for the chopsticks that support our meals and sustain our lives—this beautiful culture of prayer remains deeply rooted in this land today. A rich dining experience begins with a single pair of chopsticks. Inspired by Hashikura’s spirit of "gratitude for food and chopsticks," this pair was carefully selected to turn your everyday dining table into a special place. Feel the warmth of the wood and enjoy the "little everyday richness" passed down through 1,200 years of history.
